To define the game design of a "VOTV-like" (Voices of the Void-like), we must
break down its mechanics, pacing, and systems into abstract concepts. At its
core, a VOTV-like is an isolated, physics-driven, job-simulation game heavily
infused with immersive sim elements, entropy management, and a calendar of
authored events.

If you are designing a game in this subgenre, here is the architectural
blueprint of what makes it tick.

1. The Core Philosophy: Isolation and Routine

The emotional baseline of a VOTV-like is not inherently horror; it is isolation
and routine. The player is placed in a remote, enclosed, semi-open world where
they are the sole operator of a large facility.

  - The Routine as an Anchor: The game establishes a daily routine. The player
    must perform a job, manage their bodily needs (sleep, hunger, stamina), and
    maintain their surroundings.
  - Contrast through Disruption: Because the baseline is a mundane, predictable
    routine, any deviation—a strange noise, a broken machine, an unexpected
    event—carries massive weight. The game design relies on making the player
    comfortable with their chores so that anomalies feel genuinely disruptive.

2. The Primary Loop: Operating Complex Machinery

Instead of simple "press E to harvest" mechanics, the player’s main job must
involve operating complex, multi-step machinery.

  - Tactile & Diegetic UI: The player interacts with physical buttons, levers,
    terminals, and screens within the world, rather than floating abstract
    menus.
  - The Assembly Line of Work: The core task must be a process. For example:
    1.  Search/Calibration: Finding a target, tuning dials, aligning
        frequencies.
    2.  Acquisition: Waiting for a process to finish (which allows time for
        other chores).
    3.  Processing: Taking the raw product and passing it through refiners,
        filters, or decoders.
    4.  Packaging & Delivery: Physically putting the finished product into a
        container and shipping it off via a physical mechanism (e.g., a drone, a
        conveyor belt) to earn currency.

3. The Secondary Loop: Entropy and Maintenance

In a VOTV-like, the world is constantly degrading. Entropy is the primary
mechanical antagonist. The player cannot simply sit in their base; the game
forces them out.

  - System Degradation: Generators run out of fuel, remote infrastructure breaks
    down, servers crash, and dirt accumulates.
  - Forced Excursions: When a remote point of interest (POI) goes offline, it
    directly impacts the player's ability to do their Primary Job. The player
    must leave the safety of their home base, navigate the map, and perform
    manual repairs.
  - Light Survival Mechanics: The player has a biological upkeep cost. They must
    buy food, manage a sleep schedule, and ensure they have enough stamina to
    complete tasks.

4. World Design: The Hub and the Wilderness

The map architecture is critical to the flow of gameplay.

  - The Home Base (The Hub): A central, highly detailed location where the
    primary machinery, player bed, and storage are located. It acts as a
    sanctuary and the heart of the operation. Over time, the player customizes
    and upgrades this space, making it feel like "home."
  - The Wilderness (The Map): A large, lonely expanse surrounding the base. It
    contains scattered outposts, vital infrastructure, and hidden secrets.
  - Vulnerable Traversal: The player is given a vehicle to navigate the
    wilderness. The vehicle is useful but flawed—it can crash, run out of fuel,
    or get stuck. It is a tool that requires its own maintenance, adding to the
    physicality of the world.

5. Pacing: The Authored Event Calendar

Rather than relying on pure systemic chaos or traditional narrative cutscenes, a
VOTV-like handles narrative and atmosphere through a Calendar of Authored World
Events.

  - The Day Cycle: The game tracks time via in-game days.
  - Scheduled Anomalies: Certain days trigger specific, hand-crafted events in
    the world. These could be strange weather phenomena, mysterious objects
    appearing on the map, a temporary change in the environment, or anomalous
    entities passing through.
  - Systemic Triggers: Other events are tied to player actions, such as reaching
    a certain quota, digging up a specific spot, or processing a specific piece
    of rare data.
  - Player Autonomy during Events: Crucially, events rarely lock the player into
    a cutscene. The world continues to simulate. If a bizarre phenomenon is
    occurring outside, the player can choose to investigate it, hide from it, or
    simply ignore it and continue doing their chores.

6. Progression and Economy

The player earns currency by successfully completing their primary job and
shipping off the results. The economy serves as the motivational engine.

  - Efficiency Upgrades: Buying faster processing speeds, better radar, larger
    batteries, or automated repair systems. This allows the player to master the
    routine.
  - Tools and Utility: Buying physical tools (flashlights, climbing gear, better
    traversal vehicles) that open up new ways to explore the map.
  - Cosmetics and Personalization: A massive part of the genre's appeal is
    base-building and decoration. Spending hard-earned currency on posters,
    rugs, furniture, or tamagotchis allows players to imprint their personality
    onto the isolating environment.

7. Interaction Design: Physicality and "Jank"

A true VOTV-like embraces immersive sim DNA. The world must feel physical.

  - Physics-Based Objects: Everything is a physics object. You don't just "add
    to inventory"—you pick up a physical box, carry it, and drop it. You stack
    items to reach high places.
  - Emergent Solutions: Because tools and objects are physics-based, players can
    solve problems creatively. If a door is locked, maybe they can stack chairs
    to climb through a window.
  - Embracing the Chaos: The physics system shouldn't be overly polished. A
    little bit of unpredictability (items tumbling over, physics collisions,
    ragdolling when crashing a vehicle) adds character, humor, and stakes to the
    mundane tasks.

Summary: The Formula

To build a VOTV-like, you are building a blue-collar job simulator inside an
unpredictable, isolated sandbox.

You make the player fall in love with the quiet peace of sweeping the floor,
upgrading their machines, and drinking coffee at 3 AM. Then, you use an authored
event calendar and mechanical entropy to force them out of their comfort zone,
creating a brilliant, dynamic loop of routine, disruption, and recovery.
